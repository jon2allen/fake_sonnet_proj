**Sonnet XXVII**

When day’s long toil hath bowed my weary frame,
And rest doth call my aching limbs to lie,
Yet sleep, that thief, doth steal me not the same—
For thought, untired, doth wake me where I sigh.
From distant shores my restless fancy flies,
To where thy form doth haunt the midnight air,
And keeps mine eyes, though heavy, fixed on skies
Where naught is seen but shadows dark and bare.

Yet lo! thy shade, a lamp in night’s deep gloom,
Doth gild the void with radiance soft and bright,
As though some star had pierced the vaulted tomb,
And turned the black to silver, dim to light.
 Thus day and night in ceaseless strife I burn,
 For thee my flesh must toil, my soul must yearn.
