**Sonnet XCIX**

O fleeting bloom, thy hue doth mock my sight,
As though some cunning hand had dipped thy face
In purest gold of dawn or twilight’s grace,
Yet stole the tint from love’s own fire bright.
The rose, once proud, now pales in shameful plight,
Her crimson fled, her pride turned to disgrace;
The daisy, too, hath lost her modest trace,
And wears thy stolen kiss in borrowed light.

The hyacinth, once blue with heaven’s dye,
Now bends in grief, his azure turned to gray;
The jasmine, pale, denies the sun’s bold ray,
And all the garden mourns in silent cry.
Yet still I find no flower, though I seek,
But bears some mark of thee, both bold and meek.
