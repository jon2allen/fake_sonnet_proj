**Sonnet XXXV**

Why rail’st thou ‘gainst the wound that time hath wrought?
The purest gold doth bear some dross within,
And stars, though bright, are dimm’d by fleeting sin,
As summer’s rose must wither, frail and fraught.
No soul is free from fault, nor I from blame,
Who, justifying thee with borrowed light,
Do stain my verse to shield thee from thy plight,
And weigh thy faults as feathers ‘gainst the flame.
Yet reason yields to passion’s sweet deceit,
And love, once foe, doth plead thy cause so well
That I, thy judge, must now thy sentence seal—
A traitor to myself in this deceit.
Thus love and loss in endless strife are met,
And I must bear what I cannot forget.
