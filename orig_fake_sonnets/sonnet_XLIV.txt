**Sonnet XLIV**

O were this clay but wing’d with thought’s swift fire,
No leagues of land nor seas’ unyielding deep
Could bar my flight where thy sweet face doth keep
Its watch—ah, then no space should me retire!
Yet stand I here, a fool of earth’s cold mire,
While fancy soars where love and longing weep;
For thought, though fleet, is but a phantom leap,
And I, a slave to time’s unkind desire.

O cruel fate! To chain what should be free,
To bid the soul outstrip the laggard sun,
Yet bind the frame to this dull gravity.
What boots it then to dream what might be done?
The flesh, a weight of tears and misery,
Doth drag the heart where thought had swiftly run.
