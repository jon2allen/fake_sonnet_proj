**Sonnet LXXXI**

Shall time’s unkind hand blot your name from earth,
Or I, dissolved to dust, forget your grace?
Nay, though my bones lie cold in silent dearth,
Your fame shall rise where mortal feet have place.
The marble tombs of kings shall crumble low,
And brass inscriptions fade to ghostly shade,
Yet in my lines your virtues bright shall glow,
When all the world’s old glories are decayed.
For verse outlives the sculptor’s artful hand,
And poets’ tongues are louder than the stone;
When breath and bone to common clay are banned,
Your praise shall live where living tongues have grown.
   So take this gift, though poor my skill may be—
   You live in song, while time consumes me.
