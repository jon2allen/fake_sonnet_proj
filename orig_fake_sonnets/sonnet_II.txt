**Sonnet II**

When time hath plowed thy forehead’s fair expanse,
And furrowed deep where roses once did bloom,
When golden locks, now silvered by his dance,
Lie thin as mist upon a winter’s gloom—
Then who shall ask where all thy splendor went,
Or where the fire that lit thy wanton prime?
To point thy hollowed gaze, thy years misspent,
Were but to mock the slow-consuming crime.

Yet lo! If thou couldst lift a tender hand
And show a child whose face doth bear thy trace,
Then wert thou rich, though age doth make thee stand
Like autumn’s leaf before the wind’s embrace.
 For in his bloom thy youth doth live anew,
 And warm thy veins when winter’s breath is through.
