**Sonnet LXIX**

O fairest form where outward grace doth shine,
No art nor hand may perfect what’s complete—
The world’s gaze lingers on thy face divine,
And foes themselves must praise thy fair conceit.
Yet tongues that crown thee with such sweet renown
Soon turn their tune, and deeper sight employ,
For beauty’s veil but hides the soul’s true crown,
And deeds betray what flattering words destroy.
They spy thy mind’s rich hue, yet judge it ill,
And mar thy bloom with whispers dark and low,
As though some canker gnawed thy petals still,
And rankness tainted all thy golden show.
 But mark the cause—thy worth too freely spent,
 For common love doth breed contempt’s lament.
