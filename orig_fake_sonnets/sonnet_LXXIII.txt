**Sonnet LXXIII**

Behold in me the withered autumn’s face,
Where golden leaves, once thick, now thinly cling,
And trembling boughs, stripped bare by time’s embrace,
Do mourn the loss of spring’s sweet chorusing.
Thou see’st in me the dim and fading light,
As dusk descends and steals the day’s last hue,
Till night, that thief of all things warm and bright,
Doth wrap the world in everlasting blue.

Yet mark the embers of a dying flame,
That glow upon the ashes of my prime,
As life must yield to death’s unyielding claim,
And feed the earth that gave it breath sublime.
 This sight doth bid thee love with deeper grace,
 For time will steal what time hath lent a space.
