**Sonnet CXVIII**

As one who, surfeited with honeyed fare,
Seeks bitter herbs to stir his cloyèd taste,
Or he who, fearing sickness unaware,
Doth purge his health lest hidden ills should waste—
So I, o’erwhelm’d by thy unwearied grace,
Did turn mine eyes to shadows sharp and cold,
And, sated with thy light, sought darker place,
As though true want could make thy love less bold.

O foolish wit! To think that love might fade
By feigning scorn where none had cause to be,
Or that the heart, by artifice betray’d,
Could cure its joy with self-inflicted plea.
 Yet now I know—such cunning breeds but pain:
 For none escape the wound they make in vain.
