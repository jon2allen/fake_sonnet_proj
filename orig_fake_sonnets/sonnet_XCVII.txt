**Sonnet XCVII**

O cruel divorce! What frost hath gripped my days,
Since thou, my sun, didst steal beyond my sight.
No bloom remains where once thy radiance played,
But barren boughs in winter’s pallid light.
Yet lo, the world still wears the summer’s guise—
The fields do swell with corn, the orchards bend
With ripened gold, as though the earth defies
The grief that doth my lonely heart suspend.
But what are these but hollow, fatherless gains?
For where thou art not, all is but a show.
The lark’s sweet song is drowned in silent pains,
And leaves, though green, do tremble at the snow.
 If aught of joy the fleeting hours impart,
 ’Tis but the ghost of joy that breaks my heart.
