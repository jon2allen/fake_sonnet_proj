**Sonnet I**

O fleeting bloom, why hoard thy golden prime,
When time’s swift scythe must lay thy splendor low?
Let not thy beauty perish in its prime,
But plant thy seed where spring’s sweet breezes blow.
For thou, self-bound in mirrors of thine eyes,
Dost starve the world to feed thy hollow flame,
And in thy stingy pride, thy wealth denies
The earth its right—the heir to bear thy name.

Lo, where the rose doth blush in morning’s light,
And bids the world to share its fleeting grace,
Thou, like the miser, lock’st thy treasure tight,
And waste the wealth no hand may e’er replace.
Then yield, proud heart, or else the world shall plead—
Thou rob’st the grave, and steal’st from time thy meed.
