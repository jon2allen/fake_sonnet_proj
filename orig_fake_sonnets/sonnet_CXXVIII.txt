**Sonnet CXXVIII**

O how my soul doth ache when thou dost sit,
And strike the trembling strings with fingers light,
While I, poor fool, must stand and gaze at it,
And curse the keys that dare thy touch invite.
What bliss hath yonder quill that leaps so bold,
To dance beneath thy palm so soft, so sweet,
Whilst I, who long to kiss thy lips of gold,
Must pine in silence, dumb and incomplete!
Would I were but a fret, a peg, a wire,
To feel thy hand’s caress, thy breath so near,
To tremble ‘neath thy touch, to feed thy fire,
And steal the bliss that lips may never bear.
 Yet since these jacks are blest beyond compare,
 Give them thy touch—but leave thy kiss for me.
