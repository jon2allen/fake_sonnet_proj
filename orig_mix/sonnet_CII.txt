**Sonnet CII**

O love, though time hath dimmed thy outward flame,
Yet in my breast thy fire burns ever bright;
Not less I hold thee, though I speak thy name
With quieter tongue, nor vaunt thy stolen light.
When first thy spring did wake my sleeping lay,
Like Philomel I poured my soul in song,
But summer’s wealth now bids the music stay—
Lest common ears should weary of the throng.
For what was rare grows dull in frequent use,
And sweets too oft repeated cloy the taste;
Yet still my heart its constant feast doth choose,
Though silence guard what words might else lay waste.
Therefore I spare thee now my ceaseless art,
Lest too much praise should mar what love imparts.
